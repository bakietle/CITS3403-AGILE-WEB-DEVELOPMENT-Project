Synopsis

Dom Cobb is a skilled thief, the absolute best in the dangerous art of extraction — stealing valuable secrets from deep within the subconscious during the dream state, when the mind is at its most vulnerable. Cobb's rare ability has made him a coveted player in this treacherous new world of corporate espionage, but it has also made him an international fugitive and cost him everything he has ever loved.

Now Cobb is being offered a chance at redemption. One last job could give him his life back but only if he can accomplish the impossible — inception. Instead of the perfect heist, Cobb and his team of specialists have to pull off the reverse: their task is not to steal an idea, but to plant one.

A masterclass in storytelling and visual effects

Christopher Nolan has crafted something truly extraordinary here. The layers of the dream world are meticulously constructed, and each level carries its own visual identity. Hans Zimmer's score is haunting and perfectly complements the escalating tension. DiCaprio delivers one of his finest performances, carrying the emotional weight of the entire film on his shoulders.

Mind-bending but emotionally distant

Inception is undeniably impressive in its technical execution and ambition. The concept is brilliantly original and the action sequences are breathtaking. However, I found it difficult to emotionally connect with the characters beyond Cobb himself. The film prioritises spectacle over depth, but what spectacle it is.

Great but slightly overhyped

Don't get me wrong — this is a very good film. The zero-gravity corridor fight alone is worth the price of admission. But after the third rewatch, the cracks start to show. Some of the exposition is clunky and the film leans too heavily on explaining its own rules. Still, Nolan's ambition is commendable.
